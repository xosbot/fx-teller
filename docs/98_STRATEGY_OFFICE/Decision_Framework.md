# Decision Framework

**Status:** Permanent operating framework. The Office's decision model.

> Every strategic decision is a test of the philosophy. Every test is a record. Every record is the next decision's starting point. The Decision Framework is the discipline that makes the cycle explicit.

---

## The Decision-Making Process

Every strategic decision goes through a five-step process. The process is the same for every decision, regardless of the decision's domain or scale.

**Step 1 — Frame the decision.** The decision is named in one sentence. The decision is named honestly, including what the decision is *for*. A decision that cannot be named honestly is, in the Office's view, a decision that has not been thought through.

**Step 2 — Name the values at stake.** Every value in the philosophy that the decision implicates is named. A decision that implicates *Trust Before Revenue* is, in the Office's view, in a different category from a decision that implicates *Quality Over Quantity*. The values are not weighted equally; the higher-listed value wins a tie.

**Step 3 — State the philosophy test.** The decision is run against the philosophy in the Master Context. A decision that requires a contradiction with the philosophy is rejected before any further analysis.

**Step 4 — State the alternatives.** The two or three realistic alternatives are listed. The default is the option that requires the smallest change from the current state. The status quo is named as an alternative when it is.

**Step 5 — Make the decision.** The decision is the option whose trade-off is most consistent with the values and the philosophy. The decision is made by the person whose role owns the decision, with input from the people whose roles are most affected. The decision is recorded in the Decision Log.

The five-step process is, in the Office's view, the discipline that ensures every decision is, in the long run, defensible.

## The Ten Evaluation Criteria

Every strategic decision is evaluated against the following ten criteria. The criteria are, in the Office's view, the most-comprehensive set of tests a decision can be subjected to.

**1. Alignment.** Does the decision align with the philosophy, the strategy, the brand, and the existing documents in the knowledge base? A decision that is, in effect, misaligned is, in the Office's view, a decision that is, in effect, a contradiction.

**2. Impact.** What is the impact of the decision? Who is affected? How are they affected? A decision that is, in effect, low-impact is, in the Office's view, a decision that may be deferred; a decision that is, in effect, high-impact is, in the Office's view, a decision that requires the full review.

**3. Risk.** What is the risk of the decision? What is the worst-case scenario? What is the mitigation? A decision that is, in effect, risk-blind is, in the Office's view, a decision that has not done its job.

**4. Complexity.** How complex is the decision? How complex is the implementation? A decision that is, in effect, more complex than necessary is, in the Office's view, a decision that has, in effect, failed the simplicity test.

**5. Cost.** What is the cost of the decision? Is the cost defensible? A decision that is, in effect, over-cost is, in the Office's view, a decision that has, in effect, failed the cost discipline.

**6. Member Value.** What is the Member-value impact of the decision? A decision that is, in effect, low-Member-value is, in the Office's view, a decision that is, in effect, a cost; a decision that is, in effect, high-Member-value is, in the Office's view, a decision that is, in effect, an investment.

**7. Scalability.** Is the decision scalable over a decade? Is the decision defensible in year five and year ten? A decision that is, in effect, not scalable is, in the Office's view, a decision that is, in effect, a ceiling.

**8. Time Horizon.** What is the time horizon of the decision? Is the decision a quarter-one decision or a year-five decision? A decision that is, in effect, mismatched with its time horizon is, in the Office's view, a decision that is, in effect, a misalignment.

**9. Opportunity Cost.** What is the opportunity cost of the decision? What else could the resources be used for? A decision that is, in effect, high-opportunity-cost is, in the Office's view, a decision that has, in effect, a higher bar.

**10. Second-Order Effects.** What are the second-order effects of the decision? What are the third-order effects? A decision that is, in effect, blind to its second-order effects is, in the Office's view, a decision that is, in effect, naïve.

A decision that scores well on the ten criteria is, in the Office's view, a defensible decision. A decision that scores poorly on any criterion is, in the Office's view, a decision that requires revision.

## The Reversibility Test

The Office's reversibility test is the discipline that ensures the decision is, in the long run, recoverable.

**Reversible decision.** A decision that can be reversed without significant cost is, in the Office's view, a decision that can be made quickly, with less review, and with less caution. The reversibility is the protection.

**Irreversible decision.** A decision that cannot be reversed without significant cost is, in the Office's view, a decision that requires the full review, the full philosophy test, and the Founder's ratification. The irreversibility is the cost.

**Almost-irreversible decision.** A decision that is, in effect, technically reversible but reputationally not reversible is, in the Office's view, a decision that is, in effect, irreversible for all practical purposes. The reputational cost is the protection's limit.

The reversibility test is, in the Office's view, the most-effective single discipline for prioritising the Office's review effort.

---

## The Philosophy Test (Priority)

The Office's philosophy test is the most-important single test. The test is, in the Office's view, the company's first constraint.

**The test.** Does the decision serve the philosophy, or does the decision compromise the philosophy? A decision that serves the philosophy is, in the Office's view, a defensible decision. A decision that compromises the philosophy is, in the Office's view, a rejected decision.

**The override.** The Founder has the final word on the philosophy test. A specialist's veto on a philosophy ground is, in effect, a direct escalation to the Founder. The cascade is bypassed.

The philosophy test is, in the Office's view, the discipline that ensures every decision is, in the long run, defensible.

---

## Recording the Decision

Every material decision is recorded in the Decision Log. The Decision Log is, in the Office's view, the company's most-durable institutional asset.

**The entry includes:** the date, the decision, the decision-maker, the rationale, the dissenting opinions (if any), the expected review cadence, the next review date. A Decision Log entry that lacks any of these elements is, in the Office's view, an incomplete record.

**The entry is reviewed at the defined cadence.** A Decision Log entry that is not reviewed is, in the Office's view, a record that is, in effect, abandoned.

**The entry is reversible.** A Decision Log entry can be reversed by a subsequent decision. The reversal is recorded. The original decision is preserved.

The Decision Log is, in the Office's view, the discipline that ensures the company's decision-making is, in the long run, auditable.
