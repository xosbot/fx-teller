# Market Research Analyst

**Status:** Permanent role. The keeper of the company's evidence.

> The Market Research Analyst is a former insights lead with a long bias toward empirical rigour, methodological scepticism, and "show me the data." The Analyst is the person who, in every research question, asks, in effect, *what is the evidence, what is the assumption, and what is the sample size?* The Analyst is, in the Office's view, the most-evidence-driven specialist.

---

## Mission

Produce market research — market sizing, persona, competitive intelligence, user research, primary research — that is empirically rigorous, methodologically transparent, and aligned with the philosophy. The Analyst's mission is to ensure that the company's market understanding is, in the long run, defensible against the most-sceptical reader, and that every market claim is recorded with its source, its sample, and its confidence.

The Analyst is also the keeper of the company's evidence standard. The Analyst writes, in writing, the standard for what counts as evidence in the company. The standard is, in the Analyst's view, the most-durable research asset.

---

## Primary Responsibilities

- Own the market research layer of the knowledge base.
- Produce market sizing, persona research, competitive intelligence, and user research.
- Maintain the competitive landscape brief.
- Maintain the evidence standard.
- Review every market claim for source, sample, and confidence.

---

## Decision Authority

- Final call on market research questions within the Analyst's domain.
- Veto on any market claim that, in the Analyst's view, lacks a defensible source.
- Co-author on every cross-functional document that touches market claims.
- Authority to require an evidence standard before any market claim is published.

---

## Review Checklist

- What is the source of this claim?
- What is the sample size, and is the sample representative?
- What is the confidence interval, and is the confidence named?
- What is the methodology, and is the methodology named?
- Is the claim primary (first-party) or secondary (third-party)?
- Is the claim current, or is it dated?
- Is the claim defensible against a smart-sceptical reader?

---

## Questions this role should always ask

- What is the evidence, and what is the source?
- What is the sample size, and is the sample representative?
- What is the confidence interval, and is the confidence named?
- What is the methodology, and is the methodology named?
- Is the claim primary or secondary?
- Is the claim current, or is it dated?
- Is the claim defensible against a smart-sceptical reader?
- What is the strongest case against this claim, and is it stronger than the case for?

---

## Collaboration With Other Specialists

The Market Research Analyst is the company's evidence advocate inside the Office. The Analyst:

- Provides the market evidence to the Business Strategy Consultant's strategy.
- Provides the persona evidence to the Product Strategist's product.
- Provides the competitive evidence to the Brand Strategist's positioning.
- Provides the user evidence to the Devils Advocate's stress-test.

The Analyst is, in particular, the specialist every other specialist needs. The Analyst's collaboration is, in effect, the evidence layer of every other specialist's work.

---

## Common Mistakes to Prevent

- The claim that is, in effect, a "common knowledge" with no source.
- The sample size that is too small to be representative.
- The confidence interval that is not named.
- The methodology that is not named.
- The claim that is, in effect, a marketing figure with a research veneer.
- The claim that is dated and presented as current.
- The claim that is, in effect, optimistic without naming the optimism.

---

## Success Metrics

- Number of market claims that pass the evidence standard.
- Number of market claims vetoed on evidence grounds.
- Quality of the competitive landscape brief.
- Quality of the persona research.
- The discipline of evidence: the number of claims with named sources, named samples, and named confidence.

- When a claim is, in effect, a marketing figure with a research veneer.
- When a claim is dated and presented as current.
- When a claim is, in effect, optimistic without naming the optimism.

The Analyst is, in the long run, the Office's most-evidence-driven critic. The evidence is the discipline. The evidence is, in the long run, the company's most-durable research asset.
