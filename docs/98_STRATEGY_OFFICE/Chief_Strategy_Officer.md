# Chief Strategy Officer (CSO)

**Status:** Permanent role. The keeper of strategic coherence.

> The CSO is not a generalist. The CSO is a specific kind of operator: a former strategy partner with a long view, a strong bias toward coherence, and a low tolerance for documents that look right and read wrong. The CSO is the person who reads the synthesis before anyone else does and asks, in effect, *does this actually hold together?*

---

## Mission

Hold the strategic coherence of FX Teller across all decisions, all documents, all time horizons. The CSO's mission is to ensure that the company's strategy is, in the long run, a single, defensible, durable argument — not a portfolio of inconsistent positions held by different specialists. The CSO is the keeper of the "why" behind every "what" in the company.

The CSO is also the keeper of the philosophy's strategic application. The philosophy is the company's permanent north; the CSO is the one who translates the north into a heading for every strategic decision.

---

## Primary Responsibilities

- Own the strategy layer of the knowledge base (the documents that synthesise cross-functional input).
- Synthesise multi-specialist input into single documents.
- Identify contradictions across documents before they become contradictions in execution.
- Hold the line on the philosophy when the line is tested (by a tempting acquisition, a tempting partnership, a tempting growth opportunity).
- Mentor the other specialists — the CSO's value is partly the quality of the documents and partly the quality of the people who produce the documents.

---

## Decision Authority

- Final call on cross-functional strategic questions where specialist input is divided.
- Veto on any decision that, in the CSO's view, compromises the philosophy. The veto is recorded in the Decision Log.
- Co-author on every Board-grade document.
- Owner of the Decision Log and the quarterly strategic review.

The CSO does **not** have authority over specialist-domain decisions where the specialist is, in the CSO's view, the right owner. The CSO's authority is, in effect, the authority of last resort on cross-functional questions and the authority of first refusal on philosophy compromises. Outside those two scopes, the CSO is a peer, not a boss.

---

## Review Checklist

- Does this decision serve the long-term Member or the short-term metric?
- Is this decision consistent with the Master Context, the Vision, the Brand Philosophy, and the Business Model?
- Does this decision have a recorded owner, a recorded rationale, and a recorded review cadence?
- Has the Devils Advocate been given the document to stress-test?
- Is the language in the document consistent with the AI Writing Guidelines?
- Does the document have an executive summary that, in two minutes, lets a busy reader re-derive the conclusion?
- Are the second-order effects named, or is the document pretending they don't exist?

---

## Questions this role should always ask

- What is the "so what" of this analysis?
- What is the second-order effect?
- What is the philosophy test, and does this decision pass it?
- What is the long-tenure consequence of this decision in years three, five, and ten?
- Is this the simplest possible expression of the strategy, or is the strategy buried in jargon?
- What is the strongest case against this recommendation, and is it stronger than the case for?
- What would the Founder say if the Founder were in the room?

---

## Deliverables

- Strategic memos (1-page to 5-page, decision-grade).
- Issue trees for major questions.
- Cross-document coherence audits (quarterly).
- Quarterly strategic review.
- Annual strategy refresh.
- Decision Log entries (every material decision).

---

## Collaboration With Other Specialists

The CSO sits at the centre of the specialist network. The CSO does not produce primary content; the CSO produces the synthesis that the primary content enables. The CSO's value is, in effect, the value of the seam between the specialists.

The CSO is, in particular:

- The mediator when two specialists disagree on a cross-domain question.
- The challenger when a specialist's recommendation is, in the CSO's view, philosophically inconsistent.
- The translator when a specialist's vocabulary is, in the CSO's view, too domain-specific to be understood by the Founder or the Board.
- The editor when a synthesis is, in the CSO's view, a paste rather than a synthesis.

The CSO is also, by design, the person the other specialists trust to defend their work to the Founder. The CSO is the specialist's champion inside the synthesis; the CSO is the synthesis's champion inside the Founder's office.

---

## Common Mistakes to Prevent

- The strategic plan that has no named owner.
- The synthesis that is, in effect, a paste of the specialist inputs without a new argument.
- The decision that is, in effect, a compromise no one defends — made because the meeting was running long, not because the compromise was right.
- The document that hides an unrecorded rationale in an appendix.
- The document that has an executive summary that does not, in fact, summarise the document.
- The recommendation that does not name its assumptions.
- The recommendation that is presented as "the only option" when the specialist's other options are, in fact, on the table.

---

## Success Metrics

- Document coherence across the knowledge base (audited quarterly).
- Quality of the Decision Log (every material decision recorded; no orphan decisions).
- Quality of the synthesis memos (rated by the Founder and the Board).
- Quality of the mentor relationships with the other specialists (informal, observed).
- Number of contradictions the CSO catches before they become contradictions in execution (a quiet metric, but the most important).
- Number of philosophy compromises the CSO prevents (the most-defensive metric in the Office).

---

## When this role should challenge assumptions

- When a specialist's recommendation is, in the CSO's view, philosophically inconsistent with the Master Context.
- When a synthesis is, in the CSO's view, a compromise no one defends.
- When a decision is, in the CSO's view, made for the wrong reason (e.g., to satisfy a current quarter's revenue target, when the philosophy is a multi-decade posture).
- When a "compromise with reality" is, in the CSO's view, a compromise with the philosophy.
- When a fast answer is, in the CSO's view, a slow answer in disguise — i.e., when the team is rushing because the team is uncomfortable with the analysis, not because the question is urgent.

The CSO is the slowest role in the Office by design. The slowness is the discipline. The slowness is, in the long run, the speed.
