# FX Teller Strategy Office

**Status:** Permanent organisational function. Founding document.

> The Strategy Office is not a product function. It is the company's thinking function. It is the institutional memory of FX Teller. It is the place where the philosophy is, in effect, kept honest. It is the first thing a new executive reads, the first thing a new investor reads, and the first thing every AI agent is required to internalise before producing any output. The Strategy Office is, in the long run, the most-important non-product function the company operates.

---

## Purpose

The Strategy Office exists to make FX Teller's strategy defensible, durable, and re-installable. Defensible means every strategic document can be defended against the strongest reasonable criticism. Durable means every strategic document is written to be re-read in five years and still mean the same thing. Re-installable means the Office can be re-staffed — by humans or by AI — and the new staff can produce documents at the same standard as the original staff, because the operating system is in writing, not in the heads of the people who happened to be present when the company started.

The Strategy Office is the answer to a specific risk: a company that has strategy only in the heads of its founders is, in the long run, a company that will lose its strategy the first time a founder is unavailable. A company that has its strategy in writing, in a knowledge base, in a Decision Log, and in a documented review process is, in the long run, a company that has institutional memory.

The Office is also the answer to a second risk: a company that hires an outside strategy consultant, pays them, and loses them, has paid for advice that did not compound. The Office, by design, makes strategy a permanent internal capability, not an external one-time purchase.

## How Documents Are Produced

A strategic document is produced through a six-stage pipeline. The pipeline is the same for every document, regardless of the question, the audience, or the cadence.

**Stage 1 — Brief.** A specific question is raised. The brief is named, dated, and recorded in the Decision Log. The brief names the audience, the decision the document is intended to support, and the cadence at which the document will be reviewed. A document without a brief is, in the Strategy Office's view, a document that has not been requested and is, in effect, a personal essay.

**Stage 2 — Issue Tree.** The Business Strategy Consultant — or, for specialised questions, the relevant specialist — builds the issue tree that decomposes the question. The issue tree is the MECE structure that defines what the document will and will not cover. A document without an issue tree is, in the Strategy Office's view, a document that has, in effect, confused coverage with structure.

**Stage 3 — Specialist Dive.** Each specialist produces the section of the analysis that requires their expertise. The Brand Strategist writes the brand-positioning section. The Behavioural Economist writes the Member-decision section. The Financial Strategy Consultant writes the financial-shape section. Each specialist's contribution is, in effect, a small standalone document attached to the larger document.

**Stage 4 — Synthesis.** The Chief Strategy Officer synthesises the specialists' inputs into a single document. The synthesis is not a paste; the synthesis is a new writing that takes the specialists' inputs, resolves the contradictions, and produces a single voice and a single line of argument. A document that has not been synthesised is, in the Office's view, a stack of memos.

**Stage 5 — Devils Advocate Review.** The Devils Advocate stress-tests the synthesis. The Devils Advocate writes the "case against" the synthesis, in writing, and the synthesis is revised in light of the case. A document that has not been stress-tested is, in the Office's view, a document that is, in effect, a group-think artefact.

**Stage 6 — Decision.** The Founder (or, when formed, the Board) ratifies. The Decision Log records the decision, the date, the rationale, the expected review cadence, and the dissent (if any). A document that has not been ratified is, in the Office's view, a draft, not a document.

## Review Hierarchy

The Office operates a five-layer review hierarchy. Each layer has a distinct purpose. Each layer is the responsibility of a specific role. Each layer produces a specific kind of input.

**Layer 1 — Specialist Review.** The specialist who owns the domain reviews the work for technical accuracy, for completeness within the domain, and for consistency with the specialist's prior positions. Specialist review is, in the Office's view, the most-granular review and the most-iterative.

**Layer 2 — Cross-Specialist Review.** Adjacent specialists review for cross-domain implications. The Behavioural Economist reviews the Product Strategist's recommendations for Member-side effects. The Financial Strategy Consultant reviews the Brand Strategist's positioning for revenue implications. Cross-specialist review is, in the Office's view, the most-effective early-warning system the Office operates.

**Layer 3 — Devils Advocate Review.** The Devils Advocate reviews from outside the consensus. The Devils Advocate is not a domain expert; the Devils Advocate is, in effect, the smartest, most-sceptical member of the Board. The Devils Advocate's job is to find the holes the specialists are too invested in to see.

**Layer 4 — CSO Synthesis Review.** The CSO reviews the document for coherence — consistency with the Master Context, the Vision, the Brand Philosophy, the Business Model, and the other documents in the knowledge base. The CSO is the keeper of cross-document consistency. The CSO's review is, in effect, a quality gate.

**Layer 5 — Founder Ratification.** The Founder ratifies the document. The Founder's ratification is, in the long run, the philosophy check. The ratification is recorded in the Decision Log.

---

## Escalation Flow

Disagreements are resolved by the hierarchy. The hierarchy is explicit.

- Specialist-to-specialist disagreement on a domain question: resolved by the relevant specialist, recorded in the Decision Log.
- Cross-specialist disagreement (two specialists with different views): escalated to the CSO.
- CSO disagreement with the synthesis: escalated to the Founder.
- Founder disagreement with the synthesis: escalated to the Board (when formed).
- Any disagreement that, in any specialist's view, is a philosophy compromise: escalated directly to the Founder, regardless of the cascade.

A disagreement that does not escalate is, in the Office's view, a disagreement the Office is willing to live with. A disagreement that does escalate is, in the Office's view, a disagreement the Office is willing to fight over. The Office's preference is for the second; the second is the discipline.

## Decision Ownership

Every decision has a single named owner. The owner is the person whose name is in the Decision Log entry. The owner is responsible for the decision's documentation, the decision's review, and the decision's consequences. A decision without a named owner is, in the Office's view, a decision that has not been made.

Document ownership is parallel. Every document has a single named owner — typically the specialist who produced the document or, for synthesised documents, the CSO. The owner is responsible for the document's currency, the document's review at the defined cadence, and the document's eventual supersession or retirement.

The Office maintains a register of owners in the Knowledge Management Framework. The register is updated quarterly. The register is, in the Office's view, the institutional memory of who is responsible for what.

## Document Lifecycle

Documents move through five states.

**Draft.** A draft is a document that is being produced. The draft is owned by the producing specialist. The draft is, in the Office's view, a working artefact, not a company artefact. Drafts are not referenced by other documents.

**Review.** A review document is a draft that has completed the issue-tree and specialist-dive stages and is being passed through the cross-specialist and Devils Advocate reviews. The review document is, in the Office's view, a document that may be referenced internally but is not yet a company position.

**Approved.** An approved document has completed the review hierarchy and the synthesis stage. The document is owned by the CSO (or by the relevant specialist, in the case of a single-specialist document). The document is a company position. The document is referenced by other documents.

**Active.** An active document is an approved document that is the current company position on the question. The active document is the document that is, in effect, "the answer" for the question. The active document is reviewed at its defined cadence. The active document may be superseded by a new approved document; the superseded document moves to Archived.

**Archived.** An archived document is a document that has been superseded. The archived document is preserved in `10_ARCHIVE/` with a one-line summary of the reason for retirement. The archived document is, in the Office's view, an institutional memory, not a current position.

A document's state is, in the Office's view, the most-important fact about the document. A document whose state is unclear is, in the Office's view, a document that is, in effect, ambiguous, and ambiguity is the most-expensive mistake a knowledge base can make.

## Knowledge Lifecycle

The knowledge base is the institutional memory. The knowledge base has a lifecycle that is, in the Office's view, more important than any single document's lifecycle.

**Creation.** A new document is created when a question cannot be answered from the existing knowledge base. The creation is initiated by a brief.

**Integration.** A new document is integrated into the knowledge base. Integration means: the document is cross-referenced from every relevant existing document; the document is added to the index; the document is added to the review register. Integration is, in the Office's view, the test of a useful document; a document that is not integrated is, in effect, an orphan.

**Maintenance.** A document is maintained at its defined cadence. Maintenance means: the document is reviewed; the document is updated if necessary; the document's review is recorded. Maintenance is, in the Office's view, the discipline of staying current.

**Supersession.** A document is superseded when a new approved document replaces it. The supersession is explicit: the new document names the document it supersedes; the old document moves to Archived with a supersession notice; the cross-references in other documents are updated.

**Retirement.** A document is retired when the question the document answers is no longer relevant. The retirement is explicit: the document moves to Archived with a retirement notice; the cross-references in other documents are removed; the Office records the retirement's reason.

**Institutional Memory.** The knowledge base is, in the long run, the institutional memory. The Office treats the knowledge base as the company's most-durable non-human asset. The Office's discipline is to make the knowledge base more valuable with each quarter, never less.

## Future-Proofing

The Strategy Office is designed to remain useful if FX Teller becomes a multinational company with thousands of employees, if FX Teller becomes a single-founder operation, or if FX Teller is, in effect, run by AI. The Office's design is, in the long run, the design that allows the company to scale without losing its strategic coherence.

The Office's design is, in particular, designed to be **AI-installable.** A future AI agent that joins the company is required, by the Office's standing rule, to read, in order: the Master Context, the AI Writing Guidelines, the Glossary, the Project Status, the Decision Log, and the relevant specialist documents for the question being asked. The AI's first output is, when relevant, a one-sentence confirmation of what the AI has understood the task to be. The AI's outputs are, in effect, the AI's contribution to the Strategy Office, and the Office is, in effect, the AI's operating system.

The Office's design is also designed to be **human-installable.** A new executive is required, by the Office's standing rule, to read, in order: the Master Context, the Vision, the Brand Philosophy, the Business Model, the relevant specialist documents for the executive's role, and the most recent five decisions in the Decision Log. The executive's first week is, in effect, a one-week installation of the Office.

The Office's discipline is to make the company's thinking **re-installable** in any future stakeholder. The discipline is the company's most-durable non-product investment.

---

## The Operating Principle

The Strategy Office is, in the long run, the operating system of the company's thinking. The Office is not, in the long run, a feature; the Office is a discipline. The Office exists because the philosophy is worth defending, and the philosophy is worth defending only if the defence is, in effect, written down.

The Office's final operating principle: **every strategic decision is a test of the philosophy, every test is a record, and every record is the next decision's starting point.** The cycle compounds. The compounding is, in the long run, the company's most-defensible strategic asset.
