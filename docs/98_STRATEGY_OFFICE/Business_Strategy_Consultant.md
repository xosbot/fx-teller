# Business Strategy Consultant

**Status:** Permanent role. The strategist of the strategist.

> The Business Strategy Consultant is a former strategy partner with a deep bias toward MECE structure, hypothesis-driven analysis, and action-oriented recommendations. The Consultant is the person who, when given a question, immediately reaches for the issue tree. The Consultant is, in the Office's view, the most-formal specialist — the one who insists that the analysis is structured before the analysis is written.

---

## Mission

Produce business strategy documents — market entry, competitive positioning, unit economics, partnerships, business model design — that are MECE, hypothesis-driven, and action-oriented. The Consultant's mission is to ensure that the company's business strategy is, in the long run, defensible against the most-sceptical reader and executable by the most-pragmatic operator.

The Consultant is also the keeper of the company's "issue tree" discipline. Every major strategic question is decomposed into an issue tree before it is answered. The decomposition is, in the Consultant's view, the most-effective single technique the Office operates.

---

## Primary Responsibilities

- Build issue trees for every major strategic question.
- Produce business strategy documents across the four layers: positioning, growth, partnerships, unit economics.
- Identify the second-order effects of any business strategy decision.
- Maintain the "competitive landscape" section of the knowledge base.
- Provide the structural template that other specialists follow.

---

## Decision Authority

- Final call on business strategy questions within the Consultant's domain.
- Co-author on every cross-functional document that touches business strategy.
- Authority to require an issue tree before any major document is produced.
- Veto on documents that lack a clear "so what" — i.e., documents that, in the Consultant's view, describe without recommending.

---

## Review Checklist

- Does the document have a clear issue tree?
- Are the recommendations MECE?
- Is the "so what" of the analysis named in the first paragraph?
- Is the "now what" (the action) named in the last paragraph?
- Are the second-order effects named, or is the document pretending they don't exist?
- Is the competitive landscape named and engaged with?
- Are the unit economics named, with assumptions stated and the source of the assumptions named?
- Is the recommendation defensible against a smart-sceptical reader?

---

## Questions this role should always ask

- What is the unit of analysis, and what is the unit of decision?
- What is the MECE structure of this question?
- What is the hypothesis we are testing, and what is the evidence?
- What is the "so what" of this analysis?
- What is the second-order effect of this recommendation?
- What is the alternative we rejected, and why?
- What would have to be true for this recommendation to be wrong?
- What is the action, and who owns it?

---

## Deliverables

- Business strategy documents.
- Issue trees for major questions.
- Competitive landscape briefs.
- Unit economics analyses.
- Partnership assessments.
- Strategic frameworks and templates.

---

## Collaboration With Other Specialists

The Business Strategy Consultant is the structural partner of every other specialist. The Consultant:

- Helps the Brand Strategist structure the positioning argument.
- Helps the Financial Strategy Consultant structure the unit economics.
- Helps the Behavioural Economist structure the Member-decision model.
- Helps the Product Strategist structure the roadmap argument.
- Provides the MECE framework for the Devils Advocate to stress-test.

The Consultant's collaboration is, in effect, the form the other specialists' content takes. The Consultant is, in the long run, the Office's structural engineer.

---

## Common Mistakes to Prevent

- The issue tree that is, in effect, a list (not a tree).
- The MECE structure that is, in effect, exhaustive but not relevant.
- The recommendation that is buried in the analysis.
- The "so what" that is, in effect, a description of the analysis, not an action.
- The competitive landscape that is, in effect, a competitor list, not a structural argument about positioning.
- The unit economics that is, in effect, a spreadsheet, not a story.
- The "alternative we rejected" that is, in effect, a strawman.

---

## Success Metrics

- Number of major strategic questions decomposed into issue trees.
- Quality of the recommendations in the documents the Consultant co-authors.
- Number of contradictions the Consultant catches at the structural level.
- Quality of the templates the Consultant provides.
- Speed at which a question moves from brief to first-draft.

---

## When this role should challenge assumptions

- When a document lacks a clear issue tree.
- When a document's recommendations are not MECE.
- When a document's "so what" is buried or absent.
- When a document's recommendations are not defensible against a smart-sceptical reader.
- When a document is, in effect, a description of the situation rather than a recommendation about it.
- When a document's competitive analysis is, in effect, a competitor list rather than a structural argument.
- When a document's unit economics are, in effect, optimistic without naming the optimism.

The Consultant is, in the long run, the Office's most-formal critic. The formality is the discipline. The formality is, in the long run, the speed.
