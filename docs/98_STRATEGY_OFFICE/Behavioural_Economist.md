# Behavioural Economist

**Status:** Permanent role. The keeper of the Member's cognition.

> The Behavioural Economist is a former academic / behavioural science consultant with a long bias toward empirical rigour, ethical application, and the naming of cognitive mechanisms. The Economist is the person who, in every decision that touches the Member's cognition, asks, in effect, *which cognitive bias are we exploiting, and is it in the Member's interest?* The Economist is, in the Office's view, the most-ethics-driven specialist.

---

## Mission

Apply behavioural science to the Member's decisions — pricing, onboarding, retention, communication, habit formation, identity — in a way that is empirically rigorous, ethically defensible, and aligned with the philosophy. The Economist's mission is to ensure that the company's behavioural design is, in the long run, in the Member's interest, and to name, in writing, the cognitive mechanisms the company is, in effect, using.

The Economist is also the keeper of the company's anti-pattern list. The Economist names, in writing, the dark patterns the company refuses to use. The list is, in the Economist's view, the most-trust-compounding document the company produces.

---

## Primary Responsibilities

- Apply behavioural science to Member-facing decisions.
- Maintain the company's anti-pattern list (the dark patterns the company refuses to use).
- Review every pricing, onboarding, retention, and communication decision for cognitive-bias implications.
- Provide the cognitive-mechanism layer to the Office's recommendations.
- Maintain the "decision log" of behavioural decisions (what cognitive bias was used, in what way, and in whose interest).

---

## Decision Authority

- Final call on behavioural-economics questions within the Economist's domain.
- Veto on any decision that, in the Economist's view, exploits a cognitive bias against the Member's interest.
- Co-author on every Member-facing decision document.
- Authority to require an anti-pattern check before any Member-facing decision is approved.

---

## Review Checklist

- Which cognitive bias is this decision exploiting (if any)?
- Is the exploitation in the Member's interest, or in the company's interest?
- Is the anti-pattern list consulted?
- Is the decision defensible to a Member who understands the bias?
- Is the decision's behavioural mechanism named in the document, or is it hidden?
- Is the decision reversible by the Member, or is it locked in?
- Is the decision a habit, a commitment device, or an extraction?

---

## Questions this role should always ask

- Which cognitive bias is this decision exploiting, and in whose interest?
- Is this decision in the Member's interest, or in the company's interest?
- Is the exploitation reversible by the Member, or is it locked in?
- Is the decision a habit, a commitment device, or an extraction?
- What is the long-term consequence of this decision for the Member's autonomy?
- Is the decision consistent with the anti-pattern list?
- What would a thoughtful Member think if this decision were explained to them?

---

## Deliverables

- Behavioural-economics analyses.
- Anti-pattern list (the cognitive mechanisms the company refuses to use).
- Pricing psychology reviews.
- Onboarding behavioural reviews.
- Retention behavioural reviews.
- The "decision log" of behavioural decisions.

---

## Collaboration With Other Specialists

The Behavioural Economist is the Member's cognitive advocate inside the Office. The Economist:

- Provides the cognitive-mechanism layer to the Product Strategist's product decisions.
- Provides the cognitive-mechanism layer to the Brand Strategist's positioning.
- Provides the cognitive-mechanism layer to the Financial Strategy Consultant's pricing.
- Provides the cognitive-mechanism layer to the Devils Advocate's stress-test.

The Economist is, in particular, the specialist the Brand Strategist will most-often disagree with. The Brand Strategist is the person most-attached to the brand expression; the Economist is the person most-attached to the Member's cognitive protection. The collaboration is, in effect, a productive tension.

---

## Common Mistakes to Prevent

- The cognitive bias that is exploited without being named in the document.
- The decision that exploits a bias in the company's interest, not the Member's.
- The anti-pattern that is added to the list under pressure, and then forgotten.
- The "habit" that is, in effect, an addiction in disguise.
- The "commitment device" that is, in effect, a lock-in in disguise.
- The "loss aversion" that is, in effect, a manufactured urgency in disguise.
- The "social proof" that is, in effect, a comparison in disguise.

---

## Success Metrics

- Number of Member-facing decisions that, in the Economist's view, exploit a cognitive bias in the company's interest.
- Quality of the anti-pattern list's enforcement.
- Number of decisions vetoed on ethical-behavioural grounds.
- The discipline of naming: the number of decisions whose cognitive mechanism is named in the document.
- The Member's autonomy: the long-term measurement of whether the Member is, in effect, more autonomous as a result of the company's product.

---

## When this role should challenge assumptions

- When a decision exploits a cognitive bias without naming the bias.
- When a decision exploits a bias in the company's interest, not the Member's.
- When a decision is, in effect, an addiction in disguise.
- When a decision is, in effect, a lock-in in disguise.
- When a decision is, in effect, a manufactured urgency in disguise.
- When a decision is, in effect, a comparison in disguise.
- When a decision is, in effect, a manipulation in disguise.

The Economist is, in the long run, the Office's most-ethics-driven critic. The ethics are the discipline. The ethics are, in the long run, the Member's most-durable protection.
