# Strategic Decision Register

**Version 1.0**
**Status:** Permanent governance document. Founding register.
**Owner:** Founder, with the Strategy Office (CSO) as custodian.
**Audience:** Founder, current and future executives, board members (when formed), future investors in diligence, AI agents producing any future document.

> This document is the canonical record of every strategic decision that materially shapes FX Teller. It is the company's institutional memory. It is the first document a new executive reads after the Master Context, and the first document an AI agent consults when asked, in effect, *why did the company do it this way?* The Register is maintained throughout the life of the company. A revised Register supersedes a prior Register. A prior Register is preserved in `10_ARCHIVE/`.

---

## 1. Purpose of this Register

A company that does not record its decisions is a company that will, in the long run, repeat its decisions. A company that records its decisions in personal emails, in Slack threads, in the heads of the founders who happened to be present, is a company that has, in effect, recorded its decisions in the most-fragile medium available. A company that records its decisions in a permanent register, in a known location, in a consistent format, with named owners and review cadences, is a company that has, in effect, recorded its decisions in the most-durable medium available.

The Register is not a meeting log. A meeting log is, in effect, a transcript; a transcript is, in effect, a record of what was said, not a record of what was decided. The Register records the decisions, not the discussions. The discussions are in the source documents; the decisions are in the Register. The Register is, in the long run, the single source of truth for *what the company has decided, why, and at what cost.*

The Register is also not a brainstorming document. A brainstorming document is, in effect, a collection of ideas; a collection of ideas is, in effect, a collection of hypotheses. The Register records the decisions that survived the brainstorming. The Register is, in the long run, the record of what the company has committed to, not the record of what the company has considered.

The Register is the institutional memory of FX Teller. The Register is the discipline that makes the company's thinking defensible, durable, and re-installable in any future contributor — human or AI.

The Register is integrated with the Strategy Office (`docs/98_STRATEGY_OFFICE/`). The CSO is the custodian of the Register. The Founder is the owner. The Strategy Office's Decision Framework (`docs/98_STRATEGY_OFFICE/Decision_Framework.md`) is the operating discipline that produces the entries. The Register is, in effect, the Decision Framework's running record.

## 2. Decision Status Definitions

Every entry in the Register has a status. The status is the entry's most-important single fact. The status definitions are the discipline that makes the Register auditable.

**Proposed.** A proposal has been raised but not yet reviewed. The proposal is, in the Office's view, an idea, not a decision. A proposed entry is owned by the proposer.

**Under Review.** A proposal has been moved into the Office's review process. The proposal is being checked for philosophy alignment, commercial viability, brand alignment, operational feasibility, financial sustainability, risk, legal posture, and long-term scalability. An under-review entry is owned by the CSO.

**Approved.** A proposal has cleared the Office's review process and the Founder's ratification. The proposal is, in the Office's view, a decision. The decision is the company's position. An approved entry is owned by the named decision owner.

**Approved with Conditions.** A proposal has been approved, but the approval is conditional on the satisfaction of specific, named conditions. The conditions are recorded in the entry. An approved-with-conditions entry is owned by the named decision owner; the conditions are reviewed at the entry's review cadence.

**Deferred.** A proposal has been reviewed but not approved, with the decision explicitly deferred to a future date. The deferral is, in the Office's view, a discipline: a deferred decision is a decision not made, and the record of the deferral is the record of the discipline. A deferred entry is reviewed at the entry's review cadence.

**Rejected.** A proposal has been reviewed and rejected. The rejection is, in the Office's view, a discipline: a rejected decision is a decision the company has explicitly considered and explicitly refused. The rejection is recorded with the rationale. A rejected entry is preserved in the Register; the entry is not deleted.

**Superseded.** A prior approved decision has been replaced by a new approved decision. The superseded decision is, in the Office's view, a record of the company's evolution. The superseded decision is preserved in the Register with a cross-reference to the superseding decision.

**Retired.** A prior approved decision is no longer in force and is not superseded by a specific new decision. The retired decision is, in the Office's view, a record of the company's history. The retired decision is preserved in the Register with a note on why the decision is no longer in force.

The status taxonomy is, in the Office's view, the discipline that makes the Register's timeline legible. A reader of the Register should be able to read the status of any entry and know, in effect, whether the entry is the current position, a past position, a future possibility, or a rejected option.

## 3. Decision Record Format

Every entry in the Register uses the following format. The format is the discipline that makes the Register's entries comparable.

```
SDR-XXX  [Title]
Date:           YYYY-MM-DD
Status:         [from Section 2]
Category:       [Brand | Business Model | Product | Member Experience | Operations | Governance | Other]
Owner:          [Named role, not named person]
Affected docs:  [List of docs in the knowledge base affected by this decision]
Review cadence: [Monthly | Quarterly | Annual | 12-month | 24-month | 36-month | As needed]

Decision:
[2-4 sentence statement of the decision.]

Rationale:
[The reasoning that supports the decision. The rationale names the philosophy, the data, and the precedent the decision draws on.]

Alternatives considered:
[2-4 alternatives that were considered and the reason each was rejected.]

Benefits:
[2-4 specific benefits the decision produces.]

Risks:
[2-4 specific risks the decision creates, and the mitigation for each.]

Trade-offs:
[The explicit trade-offs the decision accepts.]

Dependencies:
[Other decisions, documents, or external events the decision depends on.]

Implementation notes:
[Operational considerations for the decision's execution.]

Current outcome:
[The decision's status as of the last review. Includes "consistent execution; no reason to revise" for stable decisions.]

Lessons learned:
[What the company has learned from the decision's execution, recorded at each review.]
```

The format is, in the Office's view, the discipline that makes the Register's entries auditable. An entry that lacks any of the fields is, in the Office's view, an incomplete record.

### Category A: Brand & Positioning

**SDR-001  Position the company as a "Lifestyle Brand for Disciplined Traders"**
- Date: 2026-07-01 (per Master Context v1.0)
- Status: Approved
- Owner: Founder
- Affected docs: Master Context, Brand Philosophy, Customer Personas, Business Model, all downstream docs
- Review cadence: 24-month

Decision: The company is positioned as a Lifestyle Brand for Disciplined Traders. The company is explicitly NOT a podcast, signal service, education company, or trading app.

Rationale: A company that has a clear "what business are we really in?" answer compounds trust over time. The lifestyle category opens the right competitive set (members' clubs, hospitality brands) rather than the wrong one (signal apps, courses). The positioning is the first test of every brand, product, and marketing decision.

Alternatives considered: (a) Generic "trading community" — too broad, no premium signal. (b) "Trading education platform" — commoditised, anti-philosophy. (c) "Premium signal service" — explicit anti-philosophy, on the wrong side of the Master Context Section 16. (d) "Trading app with community" — misframes the product as the app rather than the brand.

Benefits: durable competitive position; premium pricing enabled; founder-grade clarity; right competitive set.

Risks: the positioning requires consistent execution for years; failure to execute collapses the positioning. The positioning is also a long-horizon bet that requires founder conviction across multiple product cycles.

Trade-offs: a smaller addressable market than the mass-market alternative; a longer path to revenue scale; a higher requirement on operational discipline.

Dependencies: Brand Philosophy, Customer Personas, Business Model Section 2.

Current outcome: consistent execution; no reason to revise.

Lessons learned: the positioning has held through every subsequent decision in the Register. The positioning is, in the Office's view, the most-durable strategic commitment the company has made.

**SDR-002  Reject the signal-selling business model explicitly**
- Date: 2026-07-01 (per Master Context Section 16)
- Status: Approved
- Owner: Founder
- Affected docs: Master Context, Brand Philosophy, Revenue Architecture, Commercial Strategy
- Review cadence: 24-month

Decision: The company will never sell trade calls, signals, or predictions as a product. A Floor Call is a transparent in-the-moment expression of the Host's process, not a recommendation, target, stop, or forecast.

Rationale: A signal-selling business is structurally a dependency business. The follower's success depends on the follower's continued subscription. The company's success depends on the Member's continued discipline, which the company is, by design, training the Member to exercise without the product. The two business models are opposites. The company has chosen its model.

Alternatives considered: (a) Sell signals as a premium add-on — directly violates philosophy, would compromise brand in year one. (b) Sell signals as the primary product — anti-philosophy, anti-brand. (c) "Educate" about signals without selling them — a hair-splitting distinction Members do not respect.

Benefits: philosophical consistency; Member trust; brand defensibility; legal cleanliness (no SEBI investment-advice exposure).

Risks: a smaller addressable market than the signal-sellers; a longer customer-education cycle; pressure to "test the waters" with a small signal product in a soft quarter.

Trade-offs: revenue ceiling is lower in the near term; growth is slower than the signal-sellers.

Dependencies: Master Context Section 16, Brand Philosophy, Hosts' training.

Current outcome: consistent execution; no reason to revise.

Lessons learned: the refusal has been tested by a number of plausible-sounding proposals (a "premium" tier, a "VIP" add-on); each has been rejected. The discipline is holding.

### Category B: Business Model & Revenue

**SDR-004  Adopt the Hybrid Membership + Trading Credits revenue model**
- Date: 2026-07-01 (per Revenue Architecture Section 4)
- Status: Approved
- Owner: Founder + Financial Strategy Consultant
- Affected docs: Revenue Architecture, Service Catalogue, Product Ecosystem
- Review cadence: 12-month

Decision: The company offers a base membership (recurring) plus a Trading Credits balance (bundled transparently into the Annual fee) for premium experiences. The model is the alternative to a single-tier subscription, which would either overcharge casual Members or undercharge heavy ones.

Rationale: The hybrid serves both the long-term Member (the recurring base) and the deepening Member (the Credits) without converting the relationship into a transaction. The hybrid is the structural commitment to the long-term Member and the à la carte economy at the same time.

Alternatives considered: (a) Pure subscription — would force a single tier, underserving both ends. (b) Pay-as-you-go only — destroys the membership relationship. (c) Tiered with feature gating — anti-philosophy ("everyone gets the same Floor"). (d) Hybrid + 5+ tiers + 5 credit types (the original draft) — over-architected, philosophically noisy, operationally infeasible for a 4-person team.

Benefits: serves both Member types; preserves the membership relationship; allows premium experiences without breaking the philosophy; allows future revenue expansion without re-platforming.

Risks: Credit mechanism operational complexity; prepaid-liability on the balance sheet; potential for gaming the system.

Trade-offs: more complex than a single-tier subscription; requires Credit accounting; requires care in the Credit design (no expiry, no transfer, no purchase, no public display).

Dependencies: Master Context Section 11, Revenue Architecture, Service Catalogue, Commercial Strategy Section 7.

Current outcome: model approved; Credit architecture refined per SDR-012; implementation pending.

Lessons learned: the 7-subagent analysis (Commercial Strategy & Pricing Psychology) refined the original draft by deleting 4 of 5 credit types and imposing the no-expiry, no-transfer, no-purchase, no-public-display rules. The refinement strengthens, rather than contradicts, the original decision.

**SDR-005  Two membership tiers in v1: Monthly on-ramp + Annual spine**
- Date: 2026-07-01 (per Commercial Strategy Section 8)
- Status: Approved
- Owner: Founder + Financial Strategy Consultant
- Affected docs: Revenue Architecture, Customer Personas, Operating Model
- Review cadence: 12-month

Decision: The company ships v1 with two membership tiers — Monthly Membership (on-ramp) and Annual Membership (spine) — and a closed Founding cohort of 50–100. The Quarterly, Professional, Enterprise, and Corporate tiers are removed from v1.

Rationale: The Working Professional persona needs a low-commitment on-ramp; the Professional Trader persona is the dominant revenue line at Annual. A 4–6 person team cannot run 5+ tiers. The Founding cohort is the company's founding story. The deleted tiers (Quarterly, Professional, Enterprise, Corporate) are deferred indefinitely — not because they are bad ideas, but because v1 cannot operate them without compromising operational simplicity and philosophy.

Alternatives considered: (a) Annual-only (Soho House) — philosophically correct but operationally blocked for v1 (unproven brand in India, excludes Working Professional). (b) Five tiers (the original draft) — over-architected, philosophically noisy. (c) Hybrid + 5 tiers + 5 credit types — too complex, conflicts with operational simplicity.

Benefits: operational simplicity; philosophy consistency; the right Member filter at the right commitment level.

Risks: Annual price elasticity unknown; churn assumptions unproven at v1; the "right" Member may not be reachable in the v1 funnel.

Trade-offs: a smaller addressable market than a 5-tier model; a longer path to enterprise revenue.

Dependencies: Customer Personas (Working Professional, Professional Trader), Revenue Architecture, Operating Model.

Current outcome: model approved; pricing TBD per Founder; implementation pending.

**SDR-007  Bootstrap capital posture: no venture capital; founder capital only**
- Date: 2026-07-01 (per Master Context, Business Model, Commercial Strategy Section 12)
- Status: Approved
- Owner: Founder
- Affected docs: Master Context Section 17, Business Model Section 11, Operating Model, Commercial Strategy
- Review cadence: 12-month

Decision: The company is funded by founder capital and Member revenue only. The company does not raise venture capital. The company may consider strategic capital only on philosophy-defending terms, never growth-on-revenue terms. The Headcount Rule is in force: a role opens only when the previous quarter's gross profit per Member × Member count exceeds the role's fully-loaded annual cost by a safety factor.

Rationale: A venture-funded growth model is, in the long run, in conflict with a discipline-first philosophy. A fund that requires 10× in five years cannot coexist with a company that requires 5%-per-quarter growth, a Membership filter that rejects the wrong Members, and a House of Traders that compounds over a decade. The "small focused team" + "premium positioning" + "no mass marketing" combination is, in financial language, the bootstrapping doctrine.

Alternatives considered: (a) Series A venture round — anti-philosophy. (b) Strategic capital from a media house, broker, or fintech — would, in the long run, conflict with the discipline-first posture. (c) Smaller angel round (₹15–30 Cr) on philosophy-defending terms — possible in Year 2 or 3 if the right partner exists; deferred per the conservative posture. (d) Bootstrap (chosen) — philosophy in financial form.

Benefits: philosophy preservation; no growth-pressure on the Founder; the company's compounding is owned by the Members; the company is, in effect, free to be small.

Risks: a smaller absolute revenue base; a slower international expansion; a more constrained House of Traders timeline; the Founder is, in effect, the long-term capital backer.

Trade-offs: speed and ceiling, in exchange for the philosophy.

Dependencies: Master Context Section 17, Business Model Section 11, Operating Model, Capital Plan (to be authored).

Current outcome: posture adopted; Headcount Rule in force; Capital Plan to be authored as a separate document.

**SDR-008  The product is designed to REDUCE engagement, not maximise it**
- Date: 2026-07-01 (per Master Context Section 1, Brand Philosophy Section 2)
- Status: Approved
- Owner: Founder + Product Strategist
- Affected docs: Master Context, Brand Philosophy, Product Ecosystem, Member Experience Blueprint, Product Principles
- Review cadence: 24-month

Decision: The product is, by design, the opposite of the industry's engagement-maximisation model. The product's signal-to-noise ratio is the most-important product metric. The product is engineered to make disciplined action easier than the alternative. The product is, in effect, an anti-engagement product.

Rationale: The industry's engagement model produces Members who are more anxious, more over-trading, more dependent, and (empirically) less profitable. The company is, in effect, building the product the industry would build if the industry were paid for Member outcomes rather than for Member time-on-screen.

Alternatives considered: (a) Industry-standard engagement-maximisation — anti-philosophy. (b) "Calm" marketing veneer on engagement-maximisation — hypocrisy. (c) Anti-engagement posture (chosen) — philosophy in product form.

Benefits: Member outcomes improve; brand defensibility; legal cleanliness; long-term trust.

Risks: the anti-engagement posture is, in the short term, less monetisable than engagement-maximisation; some Members will want more engagement than the product offers; the product surface may, in some quarters, look "boring" by industry standards.

Trade-offs: short-term monetisation ceiling; Member acquisition friction in a market that expects engagement; operational discipline to resist the engagement temptation under revenue pressure.

Dependencies: Product Principles, Experience Principles, all product surfaces.

Current outcome: philosophy in force; product surfaces in development reflect the posture (audio-first, sparse notifications, no leaderboards, no streaks).

### Category C: Product

**SDR-009  Audio-first, screen-optional product posture**
- Date: 2026-07-01 (per Master Context Section 5, Brand Philosophy Section 6)
- Status: Approved
- Owner: Product Strategist
- Affected docs: Product Ecosystem, Member Experience Blueprint, Service Catalogue
- Review cadence: 12-month

Decision: The audio layer is the spine of the product. The visual surface exists to support the audio, not to replace it. A product decision that improves the visual surface at the cost of the audio experience is rejected. The Member can use the product with the screen off.

Rationale: The Member's relationship with the markets is one part of the Member's life, and the Member's life is, in the average moment, lived away from the phone. A product that is only an app is a product that has reduced the Member's possible interactions to the moments the Member happens to be at the phone. A product that is also an audio room, a watch face, a CarPlay channel, a desktop, a community, a House is a product that is present in the Member's life in the way the Member is present in the Member's life.

Alternatives considered: (a) Screen-first (industry standard) — wrong philosophy. (b) Audio-only — too narrow. (c) Audio-first + screen-optional (chosen) — philosophy in product form.

Benefits: the Member's life is integrated with the product; the Working Professional persona is served; the brand's premium posture is expressed in the product.

Risks: a smaller pool of feature surfaces; visual-product expectations unmet; the product may feel "incomplete" to Members who expect screen-rich products.

Trade-offs: a less visually rich product; a smaller design surface; more emphasis on voice and audio quality.

Dependencies: Product Ecosystem, all surface designs.

Current outcome: posture adopted; surfaces in development reflect the posture (mobile audio room, watch, CarPlay).

Risks: the naming requires Hosts to perform at the level the naming implies; the sessions require consistent quality across Hosts.

Trade-offs: a higher Host-quality bar than "podcast" implies; a more disciplined session cadence.

**SDR-012  Trading Credits: single type, bundled, no expiry, no transfer, no purchase, no public display**
- Date: 2026-07-01 (per Commercial Strategy Section 7)
- Status: Approved (refined from initial draft)
- Owner: Founder + Financial Strategy Consultant
- Affected docs: Revenue Architecture, Service Catalogue
- Review cadence: 12-month

Decision: The company operates one credit type (Trading Credits), bundled transparently into the Annual fee. Credits do not expire, cannot be transferred, cannot be gifted, cannot be purchased with real money, and are visible only to the Member (not to other Members). The four other credit types proposed in the original draft (House Credits, Reward Credits, Referral Credits, Achievement Credits) are explicitly NOT created.

Rationale: A credit mechanism is useful psychologically if redesigned; the original draft was over-architected with 5 credit types. The "moat / switching cost" framing in the original draft is dependency-laundering. The redesigned single credit expresses pre-committed Member intent ("I want to go deeper") without extracting Member consumption. The 4 not-created credit types each violated a specific philosophy (House Credits encoded class; Reward Credits were streaks in disguise; Referral Credits were transactional; Achievement Credits were casino words).

Alternatives considered: (a) 5 credit types (original draft) — over-architected, philosophically noisy. (b) 2 credit types (Trading + House) — class system in disguise. (c) Single credit, no design rules — gameable. (d) Single credit, bundled, no expiry/transfer/purchase/public-display (chosen) — philosophy in credit form.

Benefits: psychological benefit without manipulation; philosophy consistency; operational simplicity; non-gameable.

Risks: the company may want to introduce a "Reward Credit" later under revenue pressure; the 4 not-created credit types may be requested by Members or Hosts.

Trade-offs: a less feature-rich credit system than the original draft; a more restrained Premium experience.

Dependencies: Revenue Architecture Section 4, Service Catalogue Section 4, Commercial Strategy Section 7.

Current outcome: refined design approved; implementation pending; review at 12-month.

Lessons learned: the 7-subagent analysis (Commercial Strategy & Pricing Psychology) caught the "moat / switching cost" framing as dependency-laundering. The refinement is recorded.

**SDR-014  Behavioural segmentation over demographic segmentation**
- Date: 2026-07-01 (per Customer Personas Section 2)
- Status: Approved
- Owner: Founder + Market Research Analyst
- Affected docs: Customer Personas, all marketing and product surfaces
- Review cadence: 12-month

Decision: The company segments by behaviour (relationship to markets, relationship to information, relationship to community, relationship to outcomes, relationship to authority), not by demographics (age, income, geography, profession, marital status).

Rationale: Behaviour is, in the long run, more durable than demographics. The brand's marketing is more efficient when the segmentation is on the durable variables. The brand's anti-persona filter is more effective when the filter is on the behavioural variables.

## 6. Decision Governance

The Register is, in the long run, the discipline that makes the company's decision-making auditable. The governance of the Register is, in the long run, the governance of the company's decision-making.

**Who can propose.** Any Founder, executive, or specialist can propose a decision. The proposal is documented in the Register with the proposed status. The proposal is, in the Office's view, the beginning of the discipline.

**Who reviews.** The CSO reviews the proposal. The CSO may consult the relevant specialists. The CSO is the custodian of the review process; the CSO is also the keeper of the philosophy in the review.

**Who approves.** The Founder approves material decisions. The CSO approves operational decisions within the CSO's domain. The Leadership Team approves cross-functional decisions within the Leadership Team's domain. The Board (when formed) approves structural decisions (capital, governance).

**How the Strategy Office participates.** The Strategy Office is the discipline. The Office produces the analysis, surfaces the contradictions, and recommends the philosophy-defended path. The Office is, in the long run, the test of every decision.

**When Founder approval is mandatory.** Founder approval is mandatory for: any decision that affects the philosophy; any decision that affects the capital posture; any decision that affects the membership structure; any decision that affects the House of Traders; any decision that affects the international expansion; any decision that affects the Founders' role. The mandatory list is reviewed annually.

## 7. Review Cadence

The Register is reviewed at the following cadences.

**Monthly review.** The CSO reviews the Register for new entries, new statuses, and new dependencies. The monthly review is the discipline that ensures the Register is current.

**Quarterly review.** The Founder and the CSO jointly review the Register. The quarterly review is the discipline that ensures the Register is consistent with the philosophy, the strategy, and the other documents in the knowledge base.

**Annual review.** The Founder, the CSO, and (when formed) the Board review the Register as a whole. The annual review is the discipline that ensures the Register is, in the long run, the company's institutional memory.

**Decision-specific review.** Every decision has its own review cadence, defined in the decision's entry. The decision-specific review is the discipline that ensures the decision is, in the long run, current with the company's reality.

A decision whose review has elapsed is, in the Office's view, a decision that has, in effect, become historical. A decision that has become historical is, in the long run, a decision that may, in effect, need to be revisited.

## 8. Future Register Management

The Register is, in the long run, a living document. The Register is the institutional memory of the company's decision-making. The Register is, in effect, the company's most-durable non-human strategic asset.

**Version control.** The Register is versioned in git. The version discipline is, in the Office's view, the discipline that ensures the Register's history is preserved.

**Change history.** Every entry in the Register is, in the long run, a record of the decision at the time it was made. The entry is updated with the review cadences; the entry is not rewritten. The change history is the discipline that ensures the Register is auditable.

**Archiving.** Entries that are superseded or retired are moved to `10_ARCHIVE/` with a one-line note on the reason. The archived entries are, in the long run, the institutional memory of the company's evolution.

**Superseded decisions.** A superseded decision is preserved in the Register with a cross-reference to the superseding decision. The supersession is the discipline that makes the Register's timeline legible.

**Decision lineage.** Some decisions have a lineage: a decision is approved; a related decision is approved; a third decision supersedes both. The lineage is, in the long run, the discipline that makes the Register's history auditable.

**Cross-document references.** The Register is cross-referenced from every relevant document in the knowledge base. The cross-references are the discipline that makes the Register navigable.

## Conclusion

A company that records its decisions is a company that will, in the long run, know why it is the company it is. A company that does not record its decisions is a company that will, in the long run, become a different company, without noticing, one decision at a time.

The Strategic Decision Register is, in the long run, the discipline that prevents the company from becoming a different company without noticing. The Register is, in effect, the company's memory, in writing, on paper, in a known location, in a consistent format, with named owners and review cadences. The Register is, in the long run, the discipline that makes the company's thinking defensible, durable, and re-installable.

The Register records, in effect, the company's reasoning. The reasoning is, in the long run, more valuable than the decision. A future leader who understands the reasoning can adapt the decision to a future context. A future leader who has only the decision will, in the long run, be stuck with the decision.

The Register is, in the long run, the test of the company's discipline. The Register is the discipline that makes the company, in the long run, the company the philosophy was, in fact, written for.
