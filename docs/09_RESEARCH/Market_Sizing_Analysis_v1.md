## 1. POSITION

FX Teller's addressable market is not the Indian retail-trading population (millions) but the much smaller, hard-to-reach cohort of traders who have completed a market cycle, treat the activity as a profession, and will pay a premium subscription for a philosophy-first community. Base case: a sub-1,000-Member India-only brand by Year 3. Upside case: a 2,000–5,000-Member India-plus-Dubai brand by Year 5. Downside case: a 100–300-Member India-only brand for the foreseeable future.

## 2. MARKET SIZING

The Indian retail-trading market is large — NSE/BSE active demat accounts exceed ten crore — but almost none are the customer FX Teller is built for. The TAM for "premium trading memberships in India" is best read as the empirical ceiling of comparable products and adjacent memberships. Sochho House India has a Member base in the low thousands; large Indian trading educators (Power of Stocks, Prateek Singh's network) reach audiences in the hundreds of thousands but convert paying premium Members in the tens of thousands; Indian premium SaaS at the highest price points reaches paid subscribers in the tens of thousands. A defensible magnitude for FX Teller's TAM is tens of thousands, not hundreds of thousands.

The SAM — disciplined retail traders in India with ₹1L–₹50L capital, a completed market cycle, and a Working Professional or Professional Trader profile — is larger in raw count, smaller in reality. The empirical funnel: from millions of active traders, a minority has the right capital range (30–50%); of those, a minority has survived a full cycle without churn (20–30%); of those, a minority is philosophically aligned with a discipline-first, no-signals, no-gamification product (5–10%). The compounded filter produces a SAM in the low hundreds of thousands — roughly 100,000–300,000.

The SOM — the SAM reachable through referral-led growth in five years with no paid acquisition — is materially smaller. The reachable fraction is bounded by brand visibility without paid media, conversion rates, philosophical filtering, and flywheel speed. Empirically, premium-membership brands in India that have not paid for acquisition reach five-figure Member bases in five years, not six. A defensible SOM magnitude is 2,000–5,000 Members over five years, with a 1,000-Member Year 3 only if the flywheel turns earlier and harder than the industry baseline.

## 3. PERSONA ADDRESSABLE MARKET

**Professional Trader.** Full-time, discipline-first, ₹1L–₹50L capital, completed cycle — perhaps 5,000–20,000 individuals. Smallest in absolute size, most-aligned with the philosophy, slowest to convert. Most-likely to be the referrer rather than the referred.

**Working Professional.** 9-to-6 employee, mobile-first, evening and morning time-windows, meaningful capital — perhaps 50,000–200,000. The conversion workhorse: fast buyer when referred, high retention once integrated into the commute, low philosophical friction in application.

**Entrepreneur.** Founder, freelancer, or remote worker for whom trading is a philosophy-aligned side practice — perhaps 10,000–40,000. Converts slowly, retains strongly, most-likely to apply the philosophy outside trading.

**Institutional.** Too small for v1; correctly positioned for, not operated on.

**Realistic persona mix.** Year 1: Working Professional 60–70%, Entrepreneur 20–30%, Professional Trader 5–15% — Working Professional converts fastest on referral. Year 3: Professional Trader 25–35%, Working Professional 50–60%, Entrepreneur 10–20% — the writing engine and Founding cohort have produced reputation. Year 5: Professional Trader 40–50%, Working Professional 35–45% — the philosophy is the filter, the Professional Trader is the most-aligned persona at scale.

## 4. CONVERSION FUNNEL REALISM

The 1,000-Member Year 3 target, from a 20–30 design-partner base, implies a Member growth rate of roughly an order of magnitude per year for two consecutive years — not the 5% per quarter the Business Model describes. A realistic funnel: an application-to-approval ratio in the 30–50% range (the harder the filter, the slower the funnel, the higher the Member quality); a referral rate of 10–20% of retained Members per year (industry baseline, not heroic); a first-year churn in the 5–10% monthly range for Monthly and 15–25% annual for Annual, falling as the flywheel compounds.

The 5% per quarter growth target — 5% of the existing base added per quarter — is consistent with the empirical flywheel, but only from a base of several hundred Members, not 30. From 30, 5% per quarter over 8 quarters produces ~45 Members by Year 3, not 1,000. The arithmetic requires either a more aggressive referral rate in Years 1–2, or a one-time acceleration event (a viral essay, a Host transition, a Founding-cohort close). The growth assumption is the part most-exposed to the empirical reality.

## 5. COMPETITIVE CONTEXT

The Indian trading-app norm is ₹199–₹999 per month. The trading-educator norm is ₹5,000–₹50,000 per year. Members' clubs (Soho House India equivalent) are ₹50,000–₹2,00,000 per year. Audio-first subscriptions are ₹1,000–₹15,000 per year. FX Teller's premium positioning implies an Annual price in the ₹50,000–₹2,00,000 range — 5–10x the trading-app norm, 1–2x the premium educator norm. At that price point, the addressable conversion rate is empirically 5–10% of the SAM, meaning the Working Professional SAM of 100,000–200,000 produces 1,000–2,000 Members in five years, not 5,000. The premium positioning is the conversion-rate ceiling.

## 6. PUSHBACK ON ASSUMPTIONS

The 1,000-Member Year 3 cap is reachable only if the funnel is 5–10x more productive than the empirical premium-membership baseline. The "premium, premium, premium" positioning is philosophically consistent but empirically a bet against Indian retail-trader price elasticity — Indian retail traders have been trained to associate low price with accessibility, not low quality, and a 5–10x premium without an established brand is a real conversion friction. The "Dubai by Year 3" assumption is consistent with a 5,000-Member India base, not a 1,000-Member India base — a 1,000-Member India base cannot fund the Dubai entity, the Dubai Host, and the Dubai cohort without external capital, which the bootstrap posture forbids.

## 7. FAILURE MODES

Three failure modes the model is most-exposed to. (1) The addressable market is 50% smaller than the SAM implies, because the philosophical filter is harder to clear in India than in the Master Context's English-language prose. (2) The conversion rate is 50% lower than the model implies, because the premium price point and the invite-only growth posture compound into a double friction. (3) The Dubai market is more difficult to enter than the Master Context assumes, because the Dubai Indian-origin trader community is already served by a small set of well-positioned Telegram and WhatsApp groups, and the regulatory cost of a Dubai entity in 2026 is non-trivial. The most-severe risk is the combination: a smaller market, a lower conversion rate, and a slower Dubai entry compound into a brand that, in Year 5, is smaller than the philosophy implies — and a brand that is smaller than the philosophy implies is, in the long run, a brand that has stopped compounding.
