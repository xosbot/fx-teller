## 1. Executive Summary

A service catalogue matters because a service, unlike a product, is a verb as much as a noun. A product is a thing the customer uses; a service is an experience the customer receives. A product is, in the long run, a snapshot of engineering at a moment in time; a service is, in the long run, a relationship the company has with the customer over time. A product is judged by the customer's satisfaction with the thing; a service is judged by the customer's satisfaction with the relationship. The distinction is not semantic; the distinction is operational. A product is designed once and shipped. A service is designed, delivered, reviewed, refined, and, in some cases, retired. The product is the artefact. The service is the practice.

The difference between products, services, and experiences is the difference between the thing, the interaction, and the memory. A product is the Trading Floor's audio engine. A service is the Morning Floor Briefing. An experience is the Member's memory of the Morning Floor Briefing on a Tuesday in March when the market was difficult. The catalogue describes the services, but the catalogue is written in awareness that the services exist to produce experiences, and the experiences are what the Members are, in fact, paying for.

This document supports the rest of the repository in four ways. **Pricing** — the catalogue defines the service portfolio the company prices; the pricing itself is documented elsewhere. **Operations** — the catalogue defines the services the Operations function delivers; the Operating Model describes how the Operations function delivers them. **Marketing** — the catalogue defines the services the Marketing function promotes; the Customer Personas document describes the people the Marketing function promotes them to. **Product** — the catalogue defines the services the Product function supports with surfaces; the Product Ecosystem describes the surfaces the Product function supports them with. A reader who knows the catalogue knows the company's surface area; a reader who knows the surface area knows the company.

The remainder of this document is organised as follows. The Service Philosophy states the operating principles every service must satisfy. The Service Architecture groups the services into five layers. The next five sections describe each layer in detail: Core Services, Premium Services, Community Services, House of Traders Services, and AI Services. The Service Lifecycle describes the path a service takes from idea to retirement. The Service Classification describes the six categories the company uses to manage the portfolio. The Quality Standards describe the six dimensions on which every service is evaluated. The Service Decision Framework describes the six questions every new service must answer. The Future Portfolio describes the services the company is positioned for. The Conclusion and the three closing sections (Strategic Observations, Open Questions, Dependencies) tie the catalogue back to the rest of the repository.

## 2. Service Philosophy

A service is, in the brand's view, an expression of the philosophy. A service that does not satisfy the philosophy is a service the brand does not operate, regardless of its revenue, its adoption, or its competitive necessity. Six commitments govern every service in the catalogue.

**1. Reduce Noise.** A service that adds to the Member's signal-to-noise ratio is a service that ships. A service that adds noise is a service that does not. The default is removal; the bar for addition is high. A service that is theoretically interesting but practically noise is, in the brand's view, a service the brand has not yet built well, and the brand's response is to refine or to retire.

**2. Increase Discipline.** A service that makes disciplined action easier is a service that ships. A service that makes disciplined action harder is a service that does not. A service that has no effect on discipline is, in expectation, a service that has not earned its place. The Member who uses the service for a year should leave more disciplined than the Member who arrived, and the discipline should be visible in the service's design.

**3. Create Trust.** A service that compounds trust is a service that ships. A service that draws from trust is a service that does not ship, regardless of the feature it provides. A service that the company would not want a regulator, a journalist, or a disappointed Member to read about is a service that has already failed this commitment.

**4. Strengthen Community.** A service that brings Members into contact with one another, in a structured, productive way, is a service that ships. A service that isolates Members, even in service of personalisation, is a service that is reviewed. The Member who uses the service alone is using a smaller version of the service. The Member who uses the service with others is using the service as it was designed.

**5. Respect Member Time.** A service that wastes the Member's time is a service that does not ship. A service that respects the Member's time is a service that ships. A service that is reachable in the fewest possible steps is a service that has been designed well. A service that requires a long onboarding, a long commute, or a long attention span is a service that has been designed for the wrong audience.

**6. Create Long-term Value.** A service that produces value in the first month and decays is, in the brand's view, a service the company should not operate. A service that produces value in the first month, in the first year, and in the fifth year is, in the brand's view, a service the company should operate. The value of a service is the value of the service over the Member's tenure, not the value of the service in the first month.

A seventh commitment, expressed as a negative, governs the catalogue: **no service exists purely to increase revenue.** A service whose primary purpose is revenue is a service that has, in effect, confused the catalogue with a sales document. The catalogue is a service document, not a sales document. A service that cannot defend its existence on the philosophy must, in the brand's view, be removed from the catalogue, regardless of the revenue it would produce.

## 3. Service Architecture

The service catalogue is organised in five layers. Each layer has a distinct role, a distinct cadence, a distinct test, and a distinct price (the price is documented elsewhere). The layers are not, in the brand's view, a hierarchy of importance; the layers are a vocabulary for thinking about the portfolio.

**Layer 1 — Core Services.** Core Services are the services the brand's Membership is built on. A Member who cancels the Membership cancels, in effect, the Core Services. Core Services are the largest, the most-frequently-delivered, and the most-philosophically-aligned. Core Services are the steady state of the brand. A service in this layer is a service the brand could, in principle, run for a decade.

**Layer 2 — Premium Services.** Premium Services are higher-touch, lower-frequency, more-curated services. Premium Services are sold via Trading Credits or via direct purchase. Premium Services are designed to deepen the Member's relationship with the philosophy, not to broaden the brand's product line. A Premium Service that does not deepen the relationship is rejected.

**Layer 3 — Community Services.** Community Services are the services that produce the Community's compounding. A Community Service is a service that, in effect, makes the Community a Community. A Community Service that does not produce compounding is rejected. Community Services are, in the brand's view, the most-fragile layer, because the Community is the brand's most-fragile asset.

**Layer 4 — House of Traders Services.** House of Traders Services are the services the House delivers. House of Traders Services are, in the brand's view, the most-durable layer in the catalogue, because the House is the most-durable asset the brand is building. House of Traders Services are described as future possibilities, not as current commitments, until the first House is operational.

**Layer 5 — AI Services.** AI Services are the services the AI Companion will, in the long run, deliver. AI Services are positioned for, not committed to. AI Services are described as future possibilities, with the awareness that the AI's philosophy is, in the long run, the most-carefully-governed philosophy the brand will operate.

The five layers are not independent. A Core Service is, in the brand's view, the foundation that Premium, Community, House, and AI Services are built on. A Premium Service is, in the brand's view, a deepening of a Core Service. A Community Service is, in the brand's view, a social expression of a Core Service. A House of Traders Service is, in the brand's view, a physical expression of a Core Service. An AI Service is, in the brand's view, a personal expression of a Core Service. The layers reinforce each other; the catalogue is, in the long run, a portfolio of expressions of the same philosophy.

## 4. Core Services

The Core Services layer contains nine services. Each service is the daily or near-daily expression of the brand. Each service is described in five attributes: purpose, target Member, experience, business value, and future evolution. Pricing is documented elsewhere.

**Service 1 — Trading Floor (the umbrella service).**

*Purpose.* The Trading Floor is the live, audio-first environment in which the Hosts narrate the market and the Members listen, learn, and reflect. The Floor is the spine of the brand and the surface on which most of the other Core Services are delivered.

*Target Member.* All Members, with the intensity appropriate to the persona (Professional Trader: high; Working Professional: high during commute; Entrepreneur: scheduled; Institutional: high for traders).

*Experience.* Calm, professional, audio-first, in the Member's chosen surface (mobile, watch, car, desktop). The Floor is reachable in two taps or less. The Floor is, in expectation, the most-reachable and most-reliable surface the brand operates.

*Business value.* The Floor is the brand's largest recurring-revenue surface. The Floor is the surface on which the Membership fee is, in effect, justified. The Floor is also the surface at which the brand's quality is most-tested.

*Future evolution.* The Floor is, in the long run, the surface on which the AI Companion is integrated, the surface on which the in-person Floor is, in effect, mirrored, and the surface on which the international Floors are differentiated by time zone and by voice.

**Service 2 — Morning Floor Briefing.**

*Purpose.* The Morning Floor Briefing opens the day. The Briefing frames the day's setup, names the levels being watched, and sets the tone for the Member's discipline.

*Target Member.* All Members, with particular value to Members whose day begins with the markets (Professional Trader) and Members whose day begins with a commute (Working Professional).

*Experience.* Short (10-15 minutes), focused, audio-first, with a written artefact for the Member who wants to re-read. The Briefing is the Member's first encounter with the day.

*Business value.* The Briefing is, in the brand's view, the most-watched session of the day. The Briefing is the surface at which the brand's discipline bias is most-tested.

*Future evolution.* The Briefing is, in the long run, the surface on which the AI Companion's market brief is integrated.

**Service 3 — London Session.**

*Purpose.* The London Session is the live Floor during the London market open, hosted by a Host whose time zone is in or near Europe.

*Target Member.* Members in or near Europe, Members with global trading interest, Members who want a second opinion during the European session.

*Experience.* Live, audio-first, professionally narrated. The London Session is the brand's first explicit international surface.

*Business value.* The London Session is, in the brand's view, the first step in the international expansion. The London Session's quality is the test of the brand's claim to be a global product.

*Future evolution.* The London Session is, in the long run, a differentiated surface for the international Members.

**Service 4 — New York Session.**

*Purpose.* The New York Session is the live Floor during the New York market open, hosted by the most-senior Host.

*Target Member.* All Members, with the most-Members in the room of any session.

*Experience.* Live, audio-first, professionally narrated, with the most-tested voice. The New York Session is the brand's flagship.

*Business value.* The New York Session is, in the brand's view, the brand's most-watched session globally. The New York Session's quality is the test of the brand's flagship.

*Future evolution.* The New York Session is, in the long run, the surface on which the most-senior Hosts are deployed.

**Service 5 — Closing Bell.**

*Purpose.* The Closing Bell is the live Floor at the US market close. The Closing Bell debriefs the day, names the lessons, and formally closes the Floor.

*Target Member.* All Members, with particular value to Members who want to leave the day with a process rather than with an impulse.

*Experience.* Short, focused, reflective, audio-first. The Closing Bell is, in expectation, the most-reflective session of the day.

*Business value.* The Closing Bell is, in the brand's view, the last brand expression of the day. The Closing Bell's quality is the test of the brand's ability to be a quiet presence.

*Future evolution.* The Closing Bell is, in the long run, a differentiated surface for the Closing-Bell-only Members.

**Service 6 — Weekend Review.**

*Purpose.* The Weekend Review is the longer-form Floor session on the weekend. The Weekend Review reviews the week, names the patterns, and sets up the next week.

*Target Member.* Members who want a reflective weekly cadence. The Weekend Review is, in the brand's view, the most-distinctive Core Service.

*Experience.* Longer (45-60 minutes), reflective, audio-first, with a written artefact. The Weekend Review is the Member's weekly reflection practice.

*Business value.* The Weekend Review is, in the brand's view, the most-differentiated Core Service. Most competing products do not have a weekly reflective surface.

*Future evolution.* The Weekend Review is, in the long run, a differentiated surface for the long-tenured Members.

**Service 7 — Market Commentary.**

*Purpose.* The Market Commentary is the written or audio analysis of a specific market event, released between sessions.

*Target Member.* All Members who want a written or audio artefact on a specific event.

*Experience.* Short (5-15 minutes read, 5-15 minutes listen), focused, philosophically aligned, and explicitly framed as process, not prediction.

*Business value.* The Market Commentary is, in the brand's view, the most-distributed written surface. The Market Commentary is the surface at which the brand's voice is most-tested outside the Floor.

*Future evolution.* The Market Commentary is, in the long run, a personalised surface via the AI Companion.

**Service 8 — Trading Psychology Sessions.**

*Purpose.* The Trading Psychology Sessions are the dedicated sessions on the practice of being a trader: process, discipline, patience, loss, drawdown, recovery, decision-making under uncertainty.

*Target Member.* All Members, with particular value to Members in the early months of their tenure and to Members in a difficult quarter.

*Experience.* Longer (30-60 minutes), reflective, audio-first, with a written artefact. The Trading Psychology Sessions are the brand's commitment to the Member as a person, not as a portfolio.

*Business value.* The Trading Psychology Sessions are, in the brand's view, the most-distinctive Core Service in terms of brand positioning. The Sessions are the surface at which the brand's most-defended value (discipline) is most directly taught.

*Future evolution.* The Trading Psychology Sessions are, in the long run, a personalised surface via the AI Companion.

**Service 9 — Member Discussions.**

*Purpose.* The Member Discussions are the asynchronous, written, structured conversations in the Community. The Discussions are the surface at which the Community is, in effect, the Community.

*Target Member.* All Members who want to participate. The Discussions are, in the brand's view, the most-distinctive Community surface.

*Experience.* Written, structured, moderated, with a topic, a moderator, a duration, and an expected output. A Discussion is a meeting, not a chat group.

*Business value.* The Member Discussions are, in the brand's view, the surface at which the Community's quality is most-tested. The Discussions are the surface at which the brand's most-durable asset is, in effect, produced.

*Future evolution.* The Member Discussions are, in the long run, a hybrid surface with the AI Companion providing research support and the Hosts providing framing.

The nine Core Services are the spine of the catalogue. A future Member who is offered the catalogue should be able to see, in the nine services, the daily rhythm the brand offers. A future team member who is asked to defend the catalogue should be able to defend, in the nine services, the philosophy the brand is committed to.

## 5. Premium Services

The Premium Services layer contains seven services. Each service is a higher-touch, lower-frequency deepening of the Core Services. Pricing is documented elsewhere.

**Service 1 — Private Group Sessions.** A Private Group Session is a closed session for a small group of Members (typically 5-15) with a Host on a specific topic. The Session is sold via Credits. The Session is, in the brand's view, the most-distinctive Premium surface for the long-tenured Member.

**Service 2 — Expert Roundtables.** An Expert Roundtable is a discussion-format session with a Host and one or two invited external experts on a specific topic (a market event, a methodology, a domain). The Roundtable is sold via Credits. The Roundtable is, in the brand's view, the surface at which the brand's intellectual depth is most-tested.

**Service 3 — Guest Speakers.** A Guest Speaker session is a one-off session with a recognised practitioner on a topic the Guest is expert in. The session is sold via Credits or via direct purchase. The Guest Speaker is, in the brand's view, a Premium surface that produces occasional intellectual moments.

**Service 4 — Deep-Dive Workshops.** A Deep-Dive Workshop is a half-day or full-day workshop on a specific topic: process design, risk management, trading psychology, written reflection. The Workshop is sold via Credits. The Workshop is taught by a Host, by a senior Member, or by an external practitioner the brand has vetted.

**Service 5 — Professional Development.** A Professional Development service is a longer-form programme for Members who want to develop a specific professional skill: written reflection, presentation, mentorship, moderation. The service is sold via Credits. The service is, in the brand's view, the surface at which the brand's commitment to the Member as a person is most-tested.

**Service 6 — Trading Reviews.** A Trading Review is a one-to-one or small-group review of a Member's trading process. The Review is conducted by a Host or by a senior Member. The Review is sold via Credits. The Review is, in the brand's view, the surface at which the brand's commitment to the Member's growth is most-tested.

**Service 7 — Future Coaching.** A Future Coaching service is a long-form coaching relationship between a Member and a Host, with a defined cadence and a defined duration. The Coaching is sold via Credits and is, in expectation, the brand's most-premium service. The Coaching is, in the brand's view, the surface at which the brand's commitment to the Member's discipline is most-deeply tested.

The seven Premium Services are deepening surfaces. A Member who uses a Premium Service is, in expectation, a Member who has been present for at least a year. A Premium Service that is used by a Member in the first month is, in the brand's view, a Premium Service that is being marketed to the wrong Member.

## 6. Community Services

The Community Services layer contains eight services. Each service produces the Community's compounding. Each service is described by purpose and value.

**Service 1 — Member Introductions.** Member Introductions are deliberate, structured, Host-facilitated introductions between Members. The Introductions are, in the brand's view, the most-valuable single thing the Community produces. The Introductions are not a chat group; the Introductions are a meeting.

**Service 2 — Regional Chapters.** Regional Chapters are local groups of Members in a city. The Chapters meet in person, run small-format events, and host the local Floor. The Chapters are the bridge between the digital Community and the physical Community.

**Service 3 — Networking Events.** Networking Events are the structured, in-person or virtual events at which the Community meets. The Events are, in most cases, themed (a market event, a methodology, a domain) and are hosted by a Host.

**Service 4 — Accountability Circles.** Accountability Circles are small (3-5 Member) groups that meet regularly to hold each other to a defined practice (a process journal, a discipline streak, a learning commitment). The Circles are, in the brand's view, the surface at which the Community's discipline bias is most-deeply tested.

**Service 5 — Community Recognition.** Community Recognition is the public acknowledgement of a Member's contribution: a tenure mark, a discipline streak, a Community contribution, a first House visit. The Recognition is, in most cases, Hosts-nominated, community-acknowledged, and Founders-approved.

**Service 6 — Referral Program.** The Referral Program is the structured invitation by which an existing Member refers a potential Member. The Referral is, in the brand's view, the brand's most-trusted acquisition channel. The Referral is not, in the brand's view, a transactional incentive; the Referral is a relationship.

**Service 7 — Member Spotlights.** Member Spotlights are written or audio profiles of long-tenured Members: their process, their discipline, their journey in the Community. The Spotlights are, in the brand's view, the surface at which the Community's depth is most-tested.

**Service 8 — Peer Learning.** Peer Learning is the structured, Member-led, Member-attended learning that happens inside the Community. A Member who has developed a specific skill (process design, written reflection, moderation) leads a Peer Learning session for other Members. The Peer Learning is, in the brand's view, the surface at which the Community's self-sufficiency is most-tested.

The eight Community Services are the brand's most-fragile layer. A Community Service that is, in effect, neglected is a Community Service that has, in the long run, damaged the Community. A Community Service that is, in effect, well-staffed is a Community Service that has, in the long run, compounded the Community.

## 7. House of Traders Services

The House of Traders Services layer contains nine services. Each service is a possible future experience at the House. The services are described by purpose only, because the services are positioned for, not committed to, and the House itself is, in the brand's view, a future commitment.

**Service 1 — Daily Access.** Daily Access is the most-basic House service. A Member with Daily Access can visit the House during its operating hours, use the common spaces, and be in the House's atmosphere. Daily Access is, in the brand's view, the entry-level House service.

**Service 2 — Trading Pods.** Trading Pods are small, private, dedicated spaces within the House where a Member can trade in the House's atmosphere, with the Floor in the background. The Pods are, in the brand's view, the House's most-distinctive trading surface.

**Service 3 — Networking Lounge.** The Networking Lounge is the House's common area for informal Member-to-Member interaction. The Lounge is, in the brand's view, the surface at which the House's social capital is most-produced.

**Service 4 — Coffee & Food Experience.** The Coffee & Food Experience is the House's food and beverage offering, designed for quality and for the House's calm atmosphere. The Coffee & Food Experience is, in the brand's view, a small signal that the House cares about the small things.

**Service 5 — Community Events.** Community Events are the in-House events: study groups, small-format dinners, Fireside Chats with founders, in-person Floor sessions. The Events are, in the brand's view, the surface at which the House is most-distinctively a House.

**Service 6 — Weekend Meetups.** Weekend Meetups are the House's Saturday and Sunday programming: longer-form sessions, study groups, retreats. The Meetups are, in the brand's view, the surface at which the House is most-deeply experienced.

**Service 7 — Viewing Rooms.** Viewing Rooms are small, private, dedicated spaces where a small group of Members can watch a long-form event (a keynote, a market event, a Guest Speaker) together. The Viewing Rooms are, in the brand's view, the surface at which the House's most-curated moments happen.

**Service 8 — Private Rooms.** Private Rooms are bookable, dedicated spaces where a Member or a small group can hold a private conversation, a one-to-one with a Host, or a small-format meeting. The Private Rooms are, in the brand's view, the most-flexible House surface.

**Service 9 — Future Coworking.** Future Coworking is the House's possible future offering of dedicated desks and office-style infrastructure for Members who want to work in the House's atmosphere. The Future Coworking is, in the brand's view, the surface at which the House is most-closely aligned with a co-working model; the brand is positioned to consider this surface, not committed to it.

The nine House of Traders Services are positioned for, not committed to. The House is a future commitment. A service in this layer is a service the brand is willing to consider, in the long run, when the House is operational and the brand is, in effect, ready for the service.

## 8. AI Services

The AI Services layer contains eight services. Each service is a possible future capability of the AI Companion. **Service 3 — Behaviour Coach.** The AI supports the Member's discipline in real time. The Coach is, in the brand's view, the AI's most-philosophy-aligned service — a quiet, professional presence that does not interrupt and does not push.

The eight AI Services are positioned for, not committed to. The AI's philosophy is, in the long run, the most-carefully governed philosophy the brand operates. A service in this layer is a service the brand is willing to consider, in the long run, when the AI is operational and the brand is, in effect, ready for the service.

## 9. Service Lifecycle

Every service in the catalogue goes through a seven-stage lifecycle. The lifecycle is, in the brand's view, a discipline of restraint: most services proposed will not, in the long run, be built, and most services built will not, in the long run, be permanent.

**Stage 1 — Idea.** A service idea is born. The idea is, in most cases, a Hosts' observation, a Member's request, a competitor's move, or a strategic conversation. The idea is recorded. The idea is not yet a service.

**Stage 2 — Validation.** The idea is validated against the service philosophy. The validation is a written document that names the purpose, the target Member, the experience, the business value, and the philosophy test. An idea that does not pass the validation is not yet a service.

**Stage 3 — Pilot.** The validated idea is piloted. The pilot is, by design, the smallest possible expression of the idea. The pilot is, by design, a test of the philosophy, not a test of the operational scale. A pilot that does not pass the philosophy is not yet a service.

**Stage 4 — Feedback.** The pilot is reviewed with the Members, the Hosts, and the Operations function. The review is, by design, observational, not promotional. The feedback is recorded. A pilot that does not produce a positive Member outcome in feedback is not yet a service.

**Stage 5 — Launch.** The service is launched. The launch is, by design, small. The launch is, by design, announced in the Community, not in a press release. The service is launched when the philosophy is the most-tested aspect of the service.

**Stage 6 — Improve.** The launched service is in continuous improvement. The service is reviewed at the next quarterly review. The service is reviewed at the one-year mark. A service that has stopped improving has, in the brand's view, started decaying.

**Stage 7 — Retire.** A service that no longer serves the philosophy is retired. The retirement is, in the brand's view, a discipline as important as the launch. A service that is retired without ceremony is, in the brand's view, a service that has damaged the brand's relationship with the Members who used the service. A service that is retired with ceremony is, in the brand's view, a service that has honoured the Members who used the service.

The seven-stage lifecycle is, in the brand's view, the discipline of restraint. A service that is launched without the lifecycle has, in the brand's view, launched at the cost of the philosophy. A service that is retired without the lifecycle has, in the brand's view, retired at the cost of the Members. The lifecycle is, in the long run, the cost of the philosophy.

## 10. Service Classification

Every service in the catalogue is classified into one of six categories. The classification is, in the brand's view, a vocabulary for thinking about the portfolio. The categories are operational, not aspirational; each category has a distinct management cadence and a distinct test.

**Category 1 — Essential.** Essential services are the services the Membership is built on. An Essential service is a service the brand commits to maintaining, in the long run, at the highest quality the brand can sustain. Essential services are reviewed at the quarterly review against the philosophy and the operational quality. The Core Services are Essential.

**Category 2 — Premium.** Premium services are the higher-touch, lower-frequency services that deepen the Membership. A Premium service is a service the brand commits to maintaining at a quality appropriate to the Premium position. Premium services are reviewed at the quarterly review against the philosophy and the Member outcome. The Premium Services are Premium.

**Category 3 — Experimental.** Experimental services are the services the brand is testing. An Experimental service is a service the brand is not yet committed to maintaining; the Experimental service is, by definition, a candidate for retirement. Experimental services are reviewed at the monthly review against the philosophy and the operational sustainability. A service in the Experimental category is a service the brand is willing to be wrong about.

**Category 4 — Seasonal.** Seasonal services are the services the brand operates at a defined cadence: quarterly, annually, on a market event, on a Holiday. A Seasonal service is a service the brand commits to operating at the defined cadence and to retiring between cadences. Seasonal services are reviewed at the post-cadence review against the philosophy and the Member outcome. The Annual Summit, when operational, is Seasonal.

**Category 5 — Partner-led.** Partner-led services are the services the brand operates in partnership with another brand. A Partner-led service is a service the brand commits to operating only as long as the partnership serves the philosophy. Partner-led services are reviewed at the partnership-review cadence against the philosophy and the partnership health. A Partner-led service that no longer serves the philosophy is retired, with the partnership reviewed accordingly.

**Category 6 — Future.** Future services are the services the brand is positioned for but has not yet built. A Future service is a service the brand is willing to consider, in the long run, when the platform, the philosophy, and the timing are aligned. Future services are reviewed at the annual review against the philosophy and the timing. The House of Traders Services and the AI Services are Future.

The six categories give the brand a vocabulary for service decisions. A new service is classified at the validation stage. A service that is misclassified is, in the brand's view, a service that has, in effect, been promised at the wrong level. A service that is correctly classified is, in the brand's view, a service that has been promised at the level the brand can sustain.

## 11. Quality Standards

Every service in the catalogue is held to six quality standards. The standards are operational, not aspirational; each can be tested by observation of a Tuesday afternoon.

**Standard 1 — Experience.** A service that produces a Member experience the brand has committed to is a service that has met the experience standard. A service that produces a Member experience the brand has not committed to is a service that has failed the experience standard. The experience standard is, in the brand's view, the most-important standard, because the experience is what the Member is, in fact, paying for.

**Standard 2 — Reliability.** A service that is reachable when the Member expects it is a service that has met the reliability standard. A service that is unreachable when the Member expects it is a service that has failed the reliability standard. The reliability standard is, in the brand's view, the most-tested standard, because reliability is the surface at which the brand's quality is most-daily-tested.

**Standard 3 — Consistency.** A service that is delivered at the same quality across Members, across Hosts, across surfaces, across months is a service that has met the consistency standard. A service that varies in quality is a service that has failed the consistency standard. The consistency standard is, in the brand's view, the most-durable standard, because consistency is what Members renew for.

**Standard 4 — Professionalism.** A service that is delivered in the professional register is a service that has met the professionalism standard. A service that is delivered in the marketing register, the casual register, or the personal register is a service that has failed the professionalism standard. The professionalism standard is, in the brand's view, the most-defensible standard, because professionalism is the surface at which the brand's character is most-tested.

**Standard 5 — Trust.** A service that compounds trust is a service that has met the trust standard. A service that draws from trust is a service that has failed the trust standard. The trust standard is, in the brand's view, the most-fragile standard, because trust is the most-fragile asset the brand has.

**Standard 6 — Member Satisfaction.** A service that produces a Member who is, after the service, more satisfied than before the service is a service that has met the satisfaction standard. A service that produces a Member who is, after the service, less satisfied is a service that has failed the satisfaction standard. The satisfaction standard is, in the brand's view, the most-honest standard, because satisfaction is the surface at which the brand's value is most-directly measured.

The six standards are reviewed at the relevant cadences. A service that fails any standard is, in the brand's view, a service that has produced a system failure. A service that fails a standard is, in the brand's view, addressed at the system level, not at the person level.

## 12. Service Decision Framework

Every proposed service is run through the framework below. A service that fails any question is rejected, regardless of the upside.

**Question 1 — Does this solve a real problem?** A service that solves a real Member problem is a service the brand considers. A service that solves a theoretical problem is a service the brand does not consider. The problem is the test. The problem is, in the brand's view, the only legitimate starting point for a service.

**Question 2 — Does this strengthen discipline?** A service that strengthens the Member's discipline is a service the brand considers. A service that weakens the Member's discipline is a service the brand does not consider. The discipline is the test. The discipline is, in the brand's view, the most-durable Member outcome the brand produces.

**Question 3 — Does this align with philosophy?** A service that aligns with the philosophy is a service the brand considers. A service that requires a contradiction with the philosophy is a service the brand does not consider. The philosophy is the test. The philosophy is, in the long run, the most-durable constraint the brand operates.

**Question 4 — Does it deserve to exist?** A service that the brand is willing to operate, to staff, to maintain, to retire, is a service the brand considers. A service the brand is not willing to operate, to staff, to maintain, to retire, is a service the brand does not consider. The question is, in the brand's view, the most-revealing test of the service's actual strategic importance.

**Question 5 — Will Members use it repeatedly?** A service that Members will use once and abandon is, in the brand's view, a service the brand does not consider. A service that Members will use repeatedly is a service the brand considers. The repeated use is the test. The repeated use is, in the brand's view, the most-honest test of the service's value.

**Question 6 — Can Operations deliver it consistently?** A service that Operations can deliver consistently is a service the brand considers. A service that Operations cannot deliver consistently is a service the brand does not consider. The consistency is the test. The consistency is, in the brand's view, the most-operational test of the service's sustainability.

A service that passes the six questions is a service the brand is willing to consider. The consideration is then governed by the *Decision Framework* in the Vision-Mission-Values document (Section 7) and recorded in the Decision Log.

## 13. Future Portfolio

The Future Portfolio is the portfolio of services the brand is positioned for but has not yet built. The portfolio is described here so the brand has a vocabulary for the services, not as a commitment to build them.

**Service 1 — Institutional Programs.** Institutional Programs are services for institutions that want to learn from the brand's philosophy: regulator briefings, university guest lectures, exchange-sponsored education, professional development programmes. The Programs are positioned for, in the long run, as a Strategic-tier revenue stream.

**Service 2 — Trading Retreats.** Trading Retreats are multi-day, off-site, low-attendance gatherings at venues chosen for calm. The Retreats are positioned for, in the long run, as a Premium-tier experience for the long-tenured Member.

**Service 3 — Annual Summit.** The Annual Summit is a multi-day gathering of Members, Hosts, founders, and a small set of aligned guests. The Summit is positioned for, in the long run, as the brand's flagship event.

**Service 4 — Certification.** Certification is a long-form, multi-week programme that certifies a Member in a specific practice the brand teaches: process design, trading psychology, community moderation. The Certification is positioned for, in the long run, as a Premium-tier experience.

**Service 5 — Partner Experiences.** Partner Experiences are curated, in-person experiences with aligned partner brands. The Experiences are positioned for, in the long run, as a Secondary-tier service that is reviewed at the partnership-review cadence.

**Service 6 — International Houses.** International Houses are the House of Traders in international markets. The Houses are positioned for, in the long run, as the most-durable assets in the international markets.

**Service 7 — Corporate Programs.** Corporate Programs are services for small, professional teams of traders: prop desks, family offices, boutique funds. The Programs are positioned for, in the long run, as a Corporate-Membership-tier offering.

The seven Future Portfolio services are positioned for, not committed to. A service in this layer is a service the brand is willing to consider, in the long run, when the platform, the philosophy, and the timing are aligned.

## 14. Conclusion

The service portfolio is the company's most-complete expression of what the brand delivers. The portfolio is, in the long run, the operational complement to the philosophy: where the philosophy describes what the brand believes, the portfolio describes what the brand does. The portfolio is also, in the long run, the operational complement to the product: where the product describes what the brand builds, the service describes what the brand delivers.

The portfolio is designed to remain useful for many years. A Member who reads the portfolio in year one is, in the brand's view, a Member who can see the daily rhythm. A Member who reads the portfolio in year five is a Member who can see the rhythm that has compounded. A future team member who reads the portfolio is a team member who can see the portfolio's evolution. A future investor who reads the portfolio is an investor who can see the brand's most-complete surface area.

The portfolio is, in the long run, the brand's most-durable operational artefact. The portfolio is, in the brand's view, the document the company updates quarterly, reviews annually, and defends for decades. The portfolio is, in the long run, the service catalogue of a brand that intends to exist for a generation.

## Strategic Observations

The following observations emerged during the construction of the catalogue. They are offered as input to the Founder and the Leadership Team.

**Observation 1 — The catalogue is broader than the product surface.** The catalogue includes 48 services, of which the Product function directly supports fewer than half. The Community, House, and AI layers are services that have no software interface, or that have a software interface only as a small part of a larger experience. The implication is that the Operations function is, in the long run, at least as important as the Product function, because Operations is the function that delivers most of the catalogue.

**Observation 2 — The classification is a strategic instrument.** The six-category classification (Essential, Premium, Experimental, Seasonal, Partner-led, Future) is a vocabulary the brand can use to make explicit the strategic commitment level of every service. A service that is misclassified is, in effect, a service that has been over-promised or under-promised. The classification should be reviewed at the annual review and the classification should be visible to every team member.

**Observation 3 — The retirement stage is the most-untested discipline.** The seven-stage lifecycle includes a retirement stage that most companies do not, in fact, operate. The implication is that the brand's first retirements will be a test of the discipline. A service that is retired with ceremony is, in effect, a service that has honoured the Members who used the service. A service that is retired without ceremony is, in effect, a service that has damaged the brand. The retirement discipline is a leadership discipline, not an operational discipline.

**Observation 4 — Most services are layered on a small core.** Nine Core Services carry the Membership, and the Premium, Community, House, and AI Services are, in effect, deepening or expression of the Core. The implication is that the Core is the brand's most-load-bearing asset, and a Core failure is a brand failure. The Core is, in the long run, the surface the brand defends most-carefully.

**Observation 5 — The "no price in this document" rule is a discipline, not an oversight.** The catalogue describes services by purpose, target Member, experience, business value, and future evolution, but not by price. The price is documented elsewhere. The implication is that the catalogue is a service document, not a sales document. A reader who reads the catalogue should leave with a sense of the brand's service philosophy, not a sense of the brand's pricing structure. The pricing structure is, in the long run, a separate discipline.

The following questions emerged during the construction of the catalogue. They are presented to the Founder for decision.

**Question 1 — Which AI Services should be Member-facing vs Member-private?** The AI Services layer includes both Member-facing services (Session Summaries, Market Briefs) and Member-private services (Trading Journal, Behaviour Coach). The brand's policy on which services are public and which are private is, in the brand's view, a Founder decision, because the policy reflects the brand's posture on Member privacy and on the Community's relationship with the Member's process.

**Question 2 — What is the right cadence for the Annual Summit?** The Annual Summit is positioned for, but the cadence is not yet set. The Summit's cadence is, in the brand's view, a Founder decision, because the cadence reflects the brand's posture on the Member's travel, the brand's travel, and the brand's annual rhythm.

**Question 3 — Should the Retreat format be 25, 50, or 100 attendees?** The Customer Personas document referenced 25-attendee retreats. The Service Catalogue positions the Retreat for the long-tenured Member, and the brand's eventual Retreat size will depend on the brand's posture on intimacy, exclusivity, and operational scale. The size is, in the brand's view, a Founder decision.

**Question 4 — Should Partner-led services be branded FX Teller or co-branded?** The Service Classification includes Partner-led services. The brand's policy on whether the partner's brand is, in effect, equal to the brand's brand is, in the brand's view, a Founder decision, because the policy reflects the brand's posture on the partner relationship and on the Member's perception of the brand.

**Question 5 — How does the brand handle services that no longer serve the philosophy?** The Lifecycle includes a retirement stage. The brand's policy on the communication of the retirement, the timing of the retirement, the support of the affected Members is, in the brand's view, a Founder decision, because the policy reflects the brand's posture on the Members' trust and on the brand's integrity.

**Question 6 — What is the policy on services that depend on a specific Host?** Several services (Expert Roundtables, Private Group Sessions, Trading Reviews) are, in effect, Host-dependent. The brand's policy on whether a service continues if the Host leaves, retires, or is unavailable is, in the brand's view, a Founder decision, because the policy reflects the brand's posture on Host tenure and on service reliability.

**Question 7 — Should Certification be Member-only or open to non-Members?** The Future Portfolio includes Certification. The brand's policy on whether Certification is a Member benefit or a public programme is, in the brand's view, a Founder decision, because the policy reflects the brand's posture on the Member/non-Member distinction and on the brand's reach.

**Question 8 — How are services tested for anti-persona behaviour?** The Persona Decision Framework is, in the brand's view, a test for features. The brand's policy on whether the test is also applied to services is, in the brand's view, a Founder decision, because the policy reflects the brand's posture on persona discipline and on the brand's relationship with the anti-personas.
